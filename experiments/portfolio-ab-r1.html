Option A The Profile — prose, with work woven through.

A single editorial column. The page is a statement about who you are and what you do — opening with a real claim, evidence-rich, with project names dropped in-line as links rather than listed. Reads like a New Yorker bio paragraph or a Stripe Press author profile.

Strength: voice-led; a reader knows you within 90 seconds. Weakness: work doesn't have its own real estate; recruiters scanning for keywords or year-by-year experience will have to read.

— A profile of, by, and for

A decade of decisions, made measurable.

I lead an AI Decision Science practice in Dublin. I build production ML systems — not pilots, not prototypes — for clients who need them to work on a Monday morning when nobody is paying attention. The work usually involves a federal agency, a commercial firm, or an applied-research group; sometimes all three on the same engagement.

The arc, briefly. Parcel-level flood-risk scoring for a federal agency in 2025 — fourteen times the speed of the incumbent at three percentage points better recall, with calibration that held a full annual cycle. Real-time triage for emergency dispatch the year before. Synthetic demographic uplift for underrepresented cohorts in applied research; counterfactual evaluation for policy interventions in the public sector before that. The list is shorter than it sounds, because I prefer to ship one good thing than three competent ones.

Building production ML systems at planetary scale requires more than engineering. It requires deciding what to measure, what to defend, and what to leave broken on purpose.

I write about the work in a field journal — the operator at 02:00, the operator at 07:42, the death of the dashboard. I keep a commonplace book for what I've read this year; I'm currently re-reading Tufte. I'm in Dublin and am open to senior IC and director-level roles across Europe, with a strong preference for the kind of organisation that knows the difference between a model and a system.

Best by email; two-day reply window. Five-line preference: what the system does, who's already running it, what's broken about it, what you wish you'd known six months ago, what timeline you're working to.

Option B The Manifest — things shipped, in order.

A dated, reverse-chronological log of professional work. Each entry: when, what, one-line description, domain tag. The portfolio is the work — not the person. Reads like a git log, a printer's catalog, or a captain's shipping manifest.

Strength: direct, scannable; a peer can read the arc in a beat. Time-led — parallels the obsidian Score in a different register. Weakness: voice is reduced to entry copywriting; "who is this person" lives entirely in what they shipped.
