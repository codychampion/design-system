On retrieval, & the morning it failed me.

By
cc
In
operations · personal-tooling
Place
Dublin
Sound
rain on the skylight

The problem with most knowledge management isn't storage — it's retrieval under pressure. At seven in the morning, with the baby crying and standup in eight minutes, search is the wrong primitive. What you want is a system that already knows what you would have asked, and has surfaced the three things you actually need on the way to where you were already going. Search is for the rare case. The common case wants something else.

This essay began as a paragraph in a different essay, about why dashboards keep failing the people who depend on them. I cut it because it deserved its own thinking. The dashboards essay was about the operator at 02:00 — too tired, too many tabs, three monitors and a paging tool. This one is about the operator at 07:42 — coffee not done, calendar not opened, the day not yet decided. Different operator. Same failure mode.

Search is the rare case. The common case wants something else.

Three things that survive contact with morning.

When I cleared the surface of what my own personal system tries to do, I found three things that survive contact with the actual morning: what's already on fire, what I committed to do today before I knew the day would be like this, and what I will regret not having read before the first meeting. Everything else is interruption.

That third one is interesting. It is a reading queue, but it is filtered: not "what I might read," but "what I will regret not having read." The difference between the two is the difference between a feed reader and a briefing. Feed readers are libraries; briefings are letters. I want letters in the morning, even if I write them to myself the night before.

The Bloomberg Terminal model.

The reference point I keep returning to is Bloomberg, not because it is beautiful (it isn't), but because at 02:00 in a trader's life, density is a feature and not a bug. The terminal does not pretend to know what you need. It puts the world in front of you, sorted by the structures that matter for the job, and trusts that you'll use the keys you already know. It is a system that respects an expert operator. Most personal tools, including mine until recently, have the opposite shape: they assume a novice every morning.

This is the principle that shifted the design for me. Build the personal system for the operator you will be, not the one you were before you got good at this. Most of my mornings, my expert self is up before my novice self. The novice self can have the rest of the day.

What "system" actually means.

The thing I underestimated, for years, was that the value of a personal system is not the storage at all. It is the shape of the surface that meets you at 07:42. The storage matters only insofar as it can produce the right surface. Which means the surface is the design problem, and the storage is an implementation detail.

If you take that seriously, a great many of the popular knowledge tools start to look like they have solved the wrong problem competently. They have built libraries when what was needed was a desk. A library is a place to put things; a desk is a place to do work. The work, on most mornings, is making three decisions before the eighth-minute standup. The library can wait. The library is always waiting.

⌬   ⌬   ⌬

The morning that started this essay, my system did not surface what was on fire. It surfaced what was new, which is a different and much weaker thing. By the time I found the failure, the standup was three minutes in and I was apologising to the wrong people for the wrong thing. That, more than any taste-level dissatisfaction, is what set me to rewriting it. The taste was for company. The forcing function was operational. The forcing function is, every time, the better forcing function.

I am writing the new one in obsidian-mode, by which I mean: terminal-adjacent, monospace-dominant, dense, with everything that survives contact with morning visible without scrolling. It is not pretty, and pretty was never the point. It is correct, in the small set of moments that actually count.

— cc · Dublin, on a wet morning
