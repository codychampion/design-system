§ 01 — The briefAn incumbent that took 20 hours to score the country.

The federal agency we were working with had been operating a flood-risk scoring pipeline built in stages over a decade. By 2024 it was scoring 2·1 million parcels in roughly twenty hours of wall time on a 64-core cluster, with calibration that drifted seasonally and recall that fell off in the very places where a flood-risk score mattered most: low-lying parcels at the coastal margin and along urban watersheds.

The brief was unambitious in the way most production briefs are: match incumbent accuracy in a tenth of the time. We took it as cover to attempt the harder thing — exceed incumbent recall in a tenth of the time — because the operational pain was not the runtime, it was the false negatives.

"The brief was unambitious in the way most production briefs are."

§ 02 — ApproachA hybrid hydrological-plus-ML pipeline, plus a serious calibration story.

We kept the hydrological backbone — flow-accumulation, return-period reasoning, terrain-aware drainage — and replaced the parcel-level scoring head with a gradient-boosted model on engineered features, with a small neural reweighter for the long tail. The bigger surprise was infrastructure: a Rust DuckDB-based feature store, written from scratch, that took the per-parcel feature assembly from 700ms to 4ms.

§ 03 — What shippedThe numbers and what they cost.

The headline numbers — 14× speed, 3pp better recall — went to production in June 2025. The less visible win was calibration: the incumbent's seasonal drift meant operators routinely re-thresholded twice a year. The new system holds a stable threshold for at least one full annual cycle, which removed a recurring source of operator friction.

What it cost: nine months across four people, an ugly migration period where both systems ran in shadow mode, and an honest amount of evidence-building to convince a risk-averse stakeholder that gradient boosting on top of physics was not a regression to a less defensible model.

§ 04 — What I'd changeThe thing I would not do twice.

We over-invested in a custom feature store. DuckDB had grown enough by mid-2025 that the off-the-shelf path was viable; we shipped a beautiful Rust thing that no one else can maintain. Lesson: write the boring system first and only replace it when the profiler says you must.

The right model choice, second time: probably a small transformer over the spatial neighbourhood, sharing parameters across return periods. The gradient-boosted head is excellent at what it does, but it cannot transfer across geographies the way I'd want for the next deployment.

Methods. Held-out test set, n=414,802 parcels. Spatial-block CV with 50km blocks to prevent leakage. Calibration evaluated by Brier score and reliability diagrams.

Data. National elevation, parcel boundaries, claims history (2002–2024), monthly precipitation, river-gauge feed.

Reproducibility. Inference deterministic given inputs; training reproducible up to GPU non-determinism (~0.05% AUC variance).

Sensitive. Identifiers stripped; outputs aggregated to parcel level for any public-facing surface.
